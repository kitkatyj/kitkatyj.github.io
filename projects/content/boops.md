# Bunch Of Online Physics Simulations (BOOPS)

>*"It's time to take chances, make mistakes, GET MESSY!"* Ms Frizzle, Magic School Bus (1994-1997)
## Cool Physics Games
I am a big fan of interactive tools being a learning material for students. When I was starting secondary school, I was fortunate enough to be granted my first ever laptop, which was a late-2010 MacBook Pro 13-inch. In my early journeys of exploring the internet on my own, I stumbled across many interactive games that teaches various physics concepts, particularly those from [PhET Interactive Simulations](https://phet.colorado.edu). I was particularly enchanted by the ultimate physics simulation tool Phun (now called [Algodoo](https://www.algodoo.com)), and have made several [videos](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=tmRYz1eexFw) of myself playing with it. 

These experiences then gave me the inspiration and motivation to also make my very own simulator toys and games! Unfortunately I was still in secondary school, and filled with homework so that had to be postponed to another time. I was however, am able to produce a little math interactive tool called [[graph-transformer|Graph Transformer]] in 2017 which was a stepping stone towards that goal. 

## A Research Project
Fast forward to 2021. An opportunity arose between my first and second year of university when the faculty was inviting students to get involved in self-research projects, and I felt it was the perfect time for me to start on my very own development of this exciting project of mine.

My professor in charge of this was one who have taught me physics, and I felt like it was great to take what I have learned in class to produce a toy that simulates how charged particles behave and the magnetic fields they generate when moving. Hence the first two simulations that were created: [PlayCharge](https://kitkatyj.com/boops/playcharge/) and [VizField](https://kitkatyj.com/boops/vizfield/).

## PlayCharge
PlayCharge builds upon the skills I have learned from HTML5 Canvas in [[apple-project|Apple Project]], and takes it to another level by incorporating [Coloumb's Law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coulomb%27s_law)! To illustrate the forces acting on each particle in the simulation, I was drawn to how Phun/Algodoo takes free-body diagrams, by drawing arrows that grow/shrink depending on the forces acting on it. I was able to extend it to my own simulation. This plus how the particles leave trails, was also adapted from Algodoo.

Something that I find really exciting to talk about is the "New Particle" functionality. Not only does this allow you to create a new particle, but also see how the force arrows grow and shrink as you move the particle before placing it in the world! I think it really puts the idea of the charge forces really intuitively.

This alongside other small user experience features such as Shift clicking to select multiple particles, and zooming in and out were features I was able to implement right in HTML5 Canvas. I think this really opens the door for amazing capabilities.

## VizField
For this simulation, I intend to visualise the magnetic field that is generated by a moving current, which means delving into a 3D world. Now, I do not think I am that skilled in HTML5 Canvas to produce 3D simulations, so I enlisted the help of [Three.JS](https://threejs.org) to create the visualisation, which means a lot of the features I do not have to build from scratch, such as the camera controls! 

Using the [Bio-Savart Law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biot–Savart_law), we can calculate the magnetic field at a given point and draw the 3D vector using Three.JS arrows! Meanwhile Coloumb's Law is extended to calculate the electric field using spherical geometry, then converting it into cartesian geometry to plot the vector arrows.
